How this drives material selection
The model ranks all 700 compounds by Tm_K first — anything below 3000 K is eliminated immediately for re-entry use.
OxRes filters the survivors — a high-Tm compound that oxidises away in seconds is useless in plasma. Si and B fractions must be tuned.
Toughness breaks ties — among equally heat- and oxidation-resistant candidates, the one with higher mixing entropy (more elements, higher VEC) is preferred because it won't crack under acoustic loads during descent.

How this drives material selection
Unlike re-entry, hypersonic missiles endure sustained heat for minutes, not seconds — so steady-state oxidation resistance matters more than peak Tm.
The Hf–C and Ta–C binary interaction features (R_22, R_35) are especially important here — they directly encode the bonding pairs responsible for the highest Tm boosts.
The gap-flagged compounds (LitTheory_Gap_Flag=1) are priority targets — these 140 compositions are where physics theory and literature disagree most, meaning untested high performers may hide here.

How this drives material selection
Jet engines are a multi-objective problem — the model's three targets (Tm, OxRes, Toughness) must all be high simultaneously. No single target wins.
The VEC-weighted Block 3 features (R_40–69) are the most SHAP-important here because they encode how much each element contributes to the metallic bond strength — directly linked to creep resistance.
The inverse design dataset (Step 6) feeds directly into Bayesian optimisation — the ML model suggests which new composition to test next, avoiding thousands of expensive lab experiments.
